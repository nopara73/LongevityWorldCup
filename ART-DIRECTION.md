# Longevity World Cup Art Direction

This document defines the intended art direction and product identity of the Longevity World Cup website. It is a design constitution: use it to judge future UI work before choosing specific layouts, components, or code changes.

## North Star

Longevity World Cup should feel like a real sport for biological age reversal.

The site is not primarily a health blog, calculator, wellness landing page, or research archive. It is a public competition with a scoreboard, athletes, ranks, seasons, proof, prizes, and visible momentum. The product turns private longevity practice into a game people can watch, join, and argue about.

The core tension is:

- **Scientific credibility**: biomarkers, biological aging clocks, proof, rules, public methods.
- **Sport energy**: scoreboards, podiums, champions, ranks, leagues, events, prizes.
- **Human stakes**: visible athletes, age, names, faces, records, improvements.

The art direction should make those three ideas feel like one system.

## What It Should Feel Like

- **A scoreboard for time.** The primary emotional object is the number: years reduced, rank, score, days checked in, proof count, crowd count. Numbers should feel public, consequential, and hard-earned.
- **Competitive, but not childish.** It can feel game-like and bold, but it should not feel like a mobile game, casino, gimmick, or meme site.
- **Open and verifiable.** The user should feel that the leaderboard is inspectable: names link to athletes, badges have reasons, rules are nearby, docs are readable, evidence exists.
- **A little underground.** The black chrome, phoenix mark, Bitcoin-funded prize pool, self-hosted fonts, podcast/community links, and dense public tables give the brand a founder-led, internet-native, anti-institutional edge. Preserve that. Do not sand it into generic health-tech polish.
- **Optimistic without wellness softness.** The brand is about reversing age, but its tone is not spa-like, pastel, therapeutic, or lifestyle-influencer gentle. The optimism should come from measurable progress and competition.
- **Serious about data, relaxed about culture.** The biological clocks and ranking rules need rigor; the surrounding sport culture can have badges, nicknames, merch, events, challenge boards, and direct copy.

## What It Must Not Feel Like

- **Not a medical clinic.** Avoid sterile hospital blues, stock doctor imagery, diagnostic-dashboard coldness, and over-formal clinical language.
- **Not a wellness brand.** Avoid beige spa calm, soft lifestyle photography, vague transformation copy, supplement-brand gloss, and "optimize your vitality" language.
- **Not generic SaaS.** Avoid feature-card grids, sterile hero sections, blue-purple gradients, soft glass cards, tiny section eyebrows everywhere, and empty abstract illustrations.
- **Not crypto casino.** Bitcoin and prize money are part of the product, but the site should not feel speculative, flashy, gold-plated, or hype-driven.
- **Not esports neon.** The scoreboard language is useful; cyberpunk neon, glow overload, sci-fi grids, and aggressive gaming UI would make the science feel unserious.
- **Not academic-only.** Research references and docs matter, but the site should not become a journal index. The competition, athletes, and ranks must remain the first impression.
- **Not an inspirational blog.** Avoid long motivational copy before showing the leaderboard. The product proves itself through public standings.

## Core Brand Patterns

### Scoreboard Chrome

Dark chrome is the brand frame: header, hero, sticky header, footer, athlete modal, and strong entry posters. It should be near-black with subtle green/charcoal light, not flat black and not blue-gray.

Dark chrome should feel like a lit scoreboard surface. It can use:

- near-black charcoal and green-black surfaces;
- off-white text;
- bright green for scores and primary action;
- hairline borders and controlled glow;
- the phoenix mark as a large watermark or small crest.

Dark chrome should not swallow the whole site. Content sections below the hero should sit on light paper.

### Light Paper Content

Most information should live on a warm, light paper surface. This keeps the dense data readable and makes the dark chrome feel intentional.

Light content should use:

- warm off-white page background;
- near-black warm ink;
- dark green links and accents;
- restrained amber for secondary/crowd/amateur semantics;
- compact panels and tables rather than decorative section cards.

The homepage below the hero, docs pages, calculators, leaderboard tables, media pages, and application flows should stay readable and calm.

### The Score Is The Hero

The strongest visual moments should be scores:

- top score on the homepage;
- podium years reduced;
- athlete modal rank and biological age values;
- leaderboard age reduction columns;
- Longevitymaxxing days and points;
- calculator result numerals.

Large numerals should use the condensed display voice and brand green when they are core performance values. Prize money can be visible, but the years-reduced score should outrank it visually.

### Condensed Sport Typography

The display voice is condensed, uppercase, scoreboard-like, and blunt. It works for:

- brand wordmark;
- page titles;
- hero score labels;
- podium score captions;
- section headings when the section is a sport surface;
- entry posters such as the play hub.

Body text, table labels, form labels, docs, and dense operational UI should remain readable sans text. Do not put display type everywhere. The display face is a horn, not the whole orchestra.

### Public Athletes

Athlete faces are core product imagery. They are not decoration.

Preserve:

- circular profile pictures in rankings;
- athlete modal portraits;
- visible names and ranks;
- compact badge strips;
- media/contact/social links where relevant;
- proof galleries and evidence sections.

The site should show real competitors before it explains too much. A visitor should quickly understand that this is a live public field of people, not a theoretical calculator.

### Dense, Honest Tables

Tables are a brand asset here. The leaderboard, event board, challenge board, and docs tables should be dense enough to feel real.

Good density means:

- stable columns;
- clear rank hierarchy;
- predictable zebra or grouped rows;
- compact badges;
- obvious active filters;
- no hover effects that reflow rows;
- responsive mobile layouts that preserve the information hierarchy.

Do not replace real tables with oversized marketing cards where the user's job is comparison.

### Compact Badges And Icons

Badges are part of the sport language. They should feel like awards, placements, or verified attributes, not decorative stickers.

Keep badges:

- compact;
- consistent in size;
- readable on hover/focus;
- visually secondary to rank and score;
- semantically colored when needed.

Avoid random icon colors. The old cyan/pink accent mix weakens the brand. Green and amber should carry most non-semantic emphasis.

### Phoenix Mark

The phoenix is the identity symbol: rebirth, age reversal, and a new sport. It works best as:

- a small crest in chrome;
- a large low-contrast watermark in posters/heroes;
- a quiet repeated brand anchor in header/footer.

Do not over-illustrate it. The mark should feel iconic and athletic, not mascot-like.

### Direct Copy

Copy should be concrete and product-shaped:

- "Top score -22.1 years"
- "The world championship of age reversal."
- "Too old for your sport? Not this one."
- "Age Reduction"
- "Ultimate League"
- "Bortz Age"
- "Pheno Age"
- "Proof"
- "Prize"

Avoid vague wellness, generic startup value propositions, and long explanations before the user sees standings. When explanation is needed, it should be close to the data it explains.

## Preserve From Production

Production contains several important identity signals that should not be lost.

- **The product structure is right.** Homepage to podium to leaderboard to highlights to Hall of Fame to FAQ to contribution is a strong narrative: see the score, see the athletes, see the activity, understand the sport, fund the prize.
- **The public leaderboard is the product center.** The dense table, ranks, profile images, badges, sponsor/media columns, filters, and "view all athletes" affordance are core.
- **Athlete modals are essential.** Production makes the athlete feel inspectable: portrait, rank, badges, proof, charts, links, and history. The modal should remain a major product surface, not a lightweight card.
- **The podium works.** Top-three athlete cards, prize labels, years reduced, and medals communicate sport quickly.
- **Docs are valuable.** About, history, and ruleset pages make the competition credible. Keep them readable, linked, and integrated.
- **Events create aliveness.** Highlights and event boards make the site feel current and social. They should remain visible and not be hidden behind static marketing.
- **The footer link field is useful.** Social links, GitHub, rules, history, contact, podcast, and merch reinforce that this is an open public project.
- **The rough founder-led quality has value.** Do not over-polish away the sense that this is a real community and competition built in public.

## Promising In Local

The current local direction contains several strong improvements.

- **The homepage hero finally has a clear art object.** The giant "Top Score -22.1 years" scoreboard poster immediately explains the sport and gives the brand a memorable first viewport.
- **The local color discipline is stronger.** Green and amber replace production's scattered cyan/pink/Material accents and make the site feel like one brand.
- **Anton/condensed display type sharpens the sport voice.** Local headings, score numerals, and entry posters feel more like a competition than production's softer generic typography.
- **The dark chrome is more intentional.** Header, hero, footer, modal, and play poster feel related instead of incidental.
- **The light-paper body is warmer and more cohesive.** It keeps dense sections readable while avoiding sterile white SaaS.
- **The play hub poster is directionally right.** The phoenix watermark, dark surface, and blunt "Just track it" language are much closer to the desired product identity than a generic form entry page.
- **The local leaderboard explanation is better.** Naming Bortz Age and Pheno Age as blood-test aging clocks and linking to ranking rules improves trust.
- **The local modal feels more like premium competition chrome.** The darker gradient, display heading, and badge treatment are stronger than the production modal's generic gray.
- **The local CTAs have clearer hierarchy.** Bright green for primary forward action and dark/quiet secondary buttons is a good system.

## Parts That Feel Wrong Or Risky

### Production Issues

- **Cyan and pink accents feel off-brand.** They make the site feel like a generic web app or old Material UI surface. Pink media icons and cyan table headers especially conflict with the sport-scoreboard identity.
- **The old homepage hero is too timid.** It presents the logo and "Play the game" but does not immediately make the score, sport, or age-reversal concept unavoidable.
- **Some cards feel generic.** White rounded cards with soft shadows are serviceable, but when repeated without a scoreboard reason they drift toward ordinary SaaS/dashboard UI.
- **The header blur/sticky overlap can feel accidental.** Chrome should feel deliberate and crisp, not like a rendering artifact.
- **Production typography lacks enough competitive voice.** It is readable, but many headings could belong to any dashboard or nonprofit page.

### Local Issues

- **Display type can become too loud if overused.** Local is strongest when display type marks sport moments. It risks becoming shouty if applied to form labels, small card headings, or long documentation.
- **The local challenge page may look too empty with local data.** Empty or zero states need to feel intentional and current, not broken or underpopulated.
- **Local full-page screenshots suggest some pages are visually shorter or lighter than production because data differs.** Do not judge richness only by local data volume; preserve production's live density.
- **The black/green direction can become heavy.** Dark chrome should frame the competition, not turn every page into a dark-mode app.
- **Warm paper plus many cards can drift into beige product UI.** The brand survives when the scoreboard, athletes, and numbers lead. If a page becomes only paper cards, it loses edge.
- **The play poster direction is good, but it should not become a generic campaign-card template.** It works because it is an entry poster with real text and the phoenix watermark. Do not repeat the same poster/card pattern everywhere.

## Surface-Specific Guidance

### Homepage

The homepage should sell the sport in this order:

1. Scoreboard hero: the best current age reduction, athlete count, pro count, aging clocks, and direct CTA.
2. Human proof: podium with faces, years reduced, and prizes.
3. Public field: embedded leaderboard and filters.
4. Aliveness: highlights/events.
5. Legitimacy: Hall of Fame, FAQ, docs links.
6. Support: contribution, newsletter, footer.

Do not lead with abstract copy. The first screen should make the number and competition obvious.

### Leaderboard

The leaderboard should feel like the official standings of a sport.

Preserve dense rows and comparison. Improve polish only when it makes scanning easier. The sidebar/filter system should feel like league navigation, not a settings drawer. Active views must be obvious. Empty states must explain whether the view is unavailable, filtered to zero, or waiting for data.

### Athlete Modal

The athlete modal should feel like opening a competitor card plus evidence dossier.

It can be darker and more dramatic than the surrounding page. It should still be readable, scrollable, and clearly structured. Portrait, name, rank, badges, performance data, proofs, events, and links are all part of the athlete identity.

### Events And Highlights

Events are the site's pulse. They should read like a sports ticker, not a social-media feed clone.

Preserve dates, athlete links, ranks, badges, and compact row structure. Use enough color to distinguish event types, but keep the system disciplined.

### Longevitymaxxing Challenge

The challenge is related but separate from Ultimate League ranking. Its art direction can be more operational and habit-board-like, but it must still feel like Longevity World Cup.

Use the same chrome, display voice, green/amber semantics, and public leaderboard logic. Do not make it feel like a generic habit tracker app.

### Calculators And Onboarding

Calculators should feel practical, trustworthy, and connected to ranking.

Forms need less drama than the hero. Use readable body type, clear grouping, compact controls, and branded result numerals. Do not let calculator pages become medical forms with no sport context.

### Documentation Pages

Docs should preserve credibility and readability.

Use the same header/footer and restrained brand accents, but do not force hero-poster energy into long-form rules/history pages. The contents navigation, headings, tables, and links should make dense rules easier to scan.

## Decision Rules For Future Work

- If a change hides ranks, scores, athlete faces, proof, or events behind generic marketing, it is probably wrong.
- If a page could belong to any wellness startup after swapping the logo, it is wrong.
- If a page could belong to any SaaS dashboard after swapping the data, it is wrong.
- If a page uses dark surfaces for everything, it is probably too heavy.
- If a page uses light cards for everything, it is probably too soft.
- If a color is not green, amber, neutral, or a clear semantic/status color, it needs a reason.
- If a visual element does not help the user understand score, rank, proof, athlete identity, event history, or next action, remove or quiet it.
- If a redesign makes the site feel more polished but less like a public sport, reject it.
- If a redesign makes the site feel weirder but less trustworthy, reject it.
- If a redesign makes the data easier to scan and the sport easier to feel, it is probably moving in the right direction.

## One-Sentence Test

Before shipping a visual change, ask:

> Does this make Longevity World Cup feel more like the official scoreboard for the sport of age reversal?

If the answer is not clearly yes, revise the direction before changing the UI.
